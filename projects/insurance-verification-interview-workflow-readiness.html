Interview answer bank

Eight questions with honest, recruiter-ready answers.

1. What is insurance verification?

It is the pre-service process of confirming the patient, policy, date-of-service eligibility, available service-specific benefit information, patient financial information, referral or authorization requirements, and other coverage. I would document the source, date, response, unresolved items, and next owner.

2. Why is insurance verification important?

It can identify coverage or requirement problems before the visit, support clearer patient communication, reduce avoidable rework, and improve claim readiness. I would avoid promising payment because the payer makes the final claim decision.

3. What is the difference between eligibility and benefits?

Eligibility confirms whether enrollment is active for the relevant date. Benefits describe what the plan reports about coverage and financial responsibility for a service type. A patient can be eligible while the specific service remains limited, excluded, out of network, or subject to authorization.

4. What should be verified before a visit?

Patient and subscriber information, member ID, payer and plan, effective and termination dates, service date, provider and facility, service-specific benefits, deductible, copay, coinsurance, network status, referral, prior authorization, limits, other insurance, and the reference used.

5. What happens if verification is skipped?

The organization may discover inactive coverage, wrong payer order, missing authorization, benefit limits, or patient responsibility after the service. That can create denials, delayed payment, rework, confusing bills, and patient frustration.

6. How do you perform verification?

I would follow the employer-approved process: confirm identifiers and the service, use the approved payer portal or transaction tool, review the response, document the evidence, separate confirmed facts from unresolved questions, route exceptions, communicate appropriately, and recheck when a trigger occurs.

7. Active versus inactive insurance?

Active means the plan reports enrollment for the relevant period, but it does not prove every service is covered or authorized. Inactive means coverage does not apply for the date or the information does not match.

8. Which portals and software are used?

Organizations may use payer portals, clearinghouses, practice-management or EHR eligibility modules, ASC X12 270/271 transactions, Medicare HETS or MAC tools, and phone verification when permitted. I would learn the employer-approved systems rather than claim platform experience I have not earned.

Simulated case

The active plan that still produced a denial.

A simulated specialty clinic confirms active commercial coverage and marks the account verified. The system does not require separate service-benefit, referral, authorization, or date-based evidence fields. The payer later denies the claim because authorization was required for the specific service and date.

Control redesign

Build a verification system that can say “unresolved.”

Separate statuses

Track registration, eligibility, benefits, network, referral, authorization, COB, patient communication, and overall readiness independently.

Evidence and timestamps

Capture payer, plan, service, date, source, reference, response date, effective dates, financial information, and unresolved messages.

Exception ownership

Assign every unclear case an owner, deadline, appointment trigger, aging status, and escalation path.

Closed-loop feedback

Return avoidable denials to the original verification stage and verify that the control change reduces repeat defects.

Insurance verification is not a checkbox. It is an evidence, interpretation, ownership, communication, and closure workflow.
